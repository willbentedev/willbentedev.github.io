How we can honor him

James Bevel was a valuable strategist of the Civil Rights Movement, but he was also a child predator, who molested his daughter Aaralyn Mills. I propose we make a statue in front of the 16th Street Baptist Church building. His good outweighs his bad, and he still deserves recognition for the good things he did.

Rev. James Bevel was a civil rights activist and one of Martin Luther King Jr.'s most influential lieutenants in the SCLC. Born in Mississippi, Bevel was ordained as a Baptist minister in 1959 and received his Bachelor of Arts from American Baptist Theological Seminary in 1961. He was very committed to the idea that religion and the church should serve as institutions for social change. He was chairman of the Nashville Student Movement, a founding member of SNCC, and joined SCLC in 1961. He then became head of direct action and youth training specialist at SCLC.

James Bevel grew up in Mississippi, with 16 siblings, in a sharecropping family. While studying at the American Baptist Theological Seminary, he became a leader of the Nashville Student Movement, where he was trained by Rev. James Lawson in Gandhi's principles, alongside civil rights leaders John Lewis, Diane Nash, and Marion Barry. As a strong preacher, Bevel inspired many citizens to join the movement, convincing Fannie Lou Hamer to attempt voter registration.

He worked with Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., where he strategized three of SCLC's most significant achievements: the 1963 Birmingham Children's Crusade, the 1965 Selma voting rights movement, and the 1966 Chicago open housing movement. He was considered a leader or co-leader for most of the movements he was involved in.

He spent weeks developing the strategy and conducting training sessions at Birmingham's 16th Street Baptist Church, teaching students nonviolent philosophy and techniques. He directed the students in organized, peaceful marches, sending approximately 50 at a time to City Hall, to confront the Mayor about segregation. His actions improved the movement because he fixed their issue of declining support from adults, and his movements were successful.

Throughout his civil rights journey, he faced large risks and challenges. He was arrested multiple times during Freedom Rides and participated in voting rights campaigns in the South where activists encountered violence. His work made him navigate opposition from authorities, one being President Kennedy's request to stop using children in demonstrations. Despite these problems, Bevel remained committed to nonviolent direct action. He supported nonviolent protests, teaching people to avoid violence and helped stop riots sometimes. The people he worked with sometimes thought his ideas were strange, as they didn't agree with using children and didn't want him to contribute to child delinquency.
